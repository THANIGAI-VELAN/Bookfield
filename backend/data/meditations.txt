Meditations is a collection of personal writings by the Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher Marcus Aurelius, composed during his military campaigns in the 2nd century CE. Written as private reflections, it articulates Stoic principles of self-discipline, virtue, and rationality, offering one of the most enduring statements of classical Stoic philosophy.

Key facts
Author: Marcus Aurelius

Written: c. 170–180 CE

Language: Greek

Genre: Philosophy / Stoicism

Structure: 12 books of personal reflections

Composition and Context
Marcus Aurelius wrote Meditations in Greek while serving on military campaigns along the Danube frontier. The text was not intended for publication; it reads as a series of notes and exercises for moral improvement. The emperor’s position as both ruler and philosopher gives the work a distinctive introspective tone, blending duty with philosophical inquiry.

Themes and Philosophy
The work centers on Stoic ideas: the transience of life, the control of one’s judgments, and acceptance of nature’s order. Marcus urges focusing only on what is within one’s power and cultivating inner virtue over external success. His reflections illustrate applied Stoicism—philosophy as a daily practice aimed at tranquility and moral integrity.

Influence and Legacy
Meditations became a cornerstone of Stoic philosophy after its rediscovery 
during the Renaissance. It has influenced thinkers from Michel de Montaigne to modern cognitive-behavioral theorists. Its plain, practical style has also made it one of the most widely read works of classical philosophy, often cited as a guide for personal resilience and leadership.
Meditations by Marcus Aurelius

Book 2

Begin the morning by saying to yourself: I shall meet with interference, ingratitude, insolence, disloyalty, ill-will, and selfishness in people today. All of these things happen because they do not know what is good or evil.

But I have seen the beauty of good and the ugliness of evil, and I recognize that those who do wrong are like me, sharing the same nature. Therefore, I cannot be harmed by them, nor can I be angry with them.

No one can involve me in wrongdoing. I will not be harmed by others, and I will not become angry or resentful. We were born to work together, like feet, hands, and eyes.

Book 4

Do not waste what remains of your life in speculating about others, unless it concerns the common good. Thinking about what someone else is doing or why they are doing it distracts you from your own purpose.

Focus on your own actions. Ensure that what you do is just, kind, and aligned with reason.

Book 5

At dawn, when you struggle to get out of bed, remind yourself: I must go to work as a human being. What do I complain of, if I am going to do what I was born to do?

You were not made for comfort and rest alone, but for action and contribution.

Book 6

If someone does wrong, it is because they are mistaken. Teach them if you can; if not, bear with them. The best revenge is to not be like them.

Book 7
Do not act as if you were going to live forever. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good.

Book 8
The happiness of your life depends upon the quality of your thoughts. Therefore, guard your mind carefully.

Reject negative thoughts and focus on what is true and meaningful.
